Real Estate

Real estate provides income, potential appreciation, and stability. This guide breaks down physical property, REITs, risks, and how real estate fits into a balanced portfolio.

What It Is

Real estate includes physical property ‚Äî homes, rentals, apartments, storage units ‚Äî AND REITs (Real Estate Investment Trusts), which let you invest in real estate without owning property directly.

Whether physical or digital, real estate often behaves differently than stocks, adding stability and income to long-term portfolios.

How It Works

Real estate builds wealth in three main ways:

  • Rental Income: Tenants pay you monthly cash flow.
  • Appreciation: Property values rise over long periods.
  • REIT Dividends: REIT companies pay out income regularly.

Real estate is income-focused. Even when prices dip, rental income often continues.

Pros

  • ‚úî Provides steady income through rent or REIT dividends
  • ‚úî Often less correlated with stock market volatility
  • ‚úî Strong long-term appreciation in many markets
  • ‚úî REITs offer real estate exposure without maintenance
  • ‚úî Helps diversify a stock-heavy portfolio

Cons

  • ‚úò Physical property requires maintenance, taxes, and management
  • ‚úò Illiquid ‚Äî property cannot be sold instantly
  • ‚úò REITs can still be affected by interest rates
  • ‚úò Leverage (mortgages) increases risk

Real estate is more stable than stocks but still reacts to interest rates, regional demand, and economic cycles. REITs fluctuate like stocks but provide consistent income.

Main Types of Real Estate Investments

  • Physical Property: Single-family homes, apartments, storage units, land.
  • Equity REITs: Own and operate properties (most common).
  • Mortgage REITs: Invest in loans, more volatile.
  • Hybrid REITs: Mix of property + mortgages.
